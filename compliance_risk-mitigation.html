WORKPLACE ETHICS

COURSE OVERVIEW
Professionalism is the conduct, aims, or qualities that characterize a professional person—it also implies quality of workmanship or service. A professional reputation is the difference between success and failure, and employers usually seek to retain their most professional team members. Those who exhibit professionalism, even under the most challenging circumstances, will have doors opening for them in the workplace and in their personal ambitions.

This course outlines the rules to being professional through the use of Soft Skills. We know that developing Soft Skills is crucial for promoting one’s success in the workplace. Therefore, our goal is to ensure that these skills are properly formulated and that adversarial attitudes toward work have been pragmatically addressed.

In this course, students learn how to gain the trust and respect of those confiding in them, and increase their influence within the organization where they are employed. More importantly, we teach students how to apply the golden rules of professionalism so they can enjoy a wonderful, professional, and prosperous career with an employer of choice.

AT THE END OF THIS COURSE, THE STUDENT SHOULD BE ABLE TO:
Explain and demonstrate ethical workplace behavior.
Exhibit Interpersonal Skills.
Understand the importance of practicing civility in the workplace.
Identify and manage one's anger.
Establish appropriate workplace connections.

EMPLOYMENT DISCRIMINATION

COURSE OVERVIEW
Employment practices that are considered to be discriminatory involve any biased behavior in employee recruiting, hiring, job assignment, compensation, promotion, setting wages and compensation, testing, training, apprenticeships, internships, termination, and various other types of discrimination that are based on these protected classifications.

Employment discrimination is likely to occur silently in the beliefs, attitudes, and values that one applies in his mind toward a candidate. Although discriminatory words may never be uttered, one who is prone to discriminate will find a non-discriminatory way to reject the candidate. The goal of eliminating employment discrimination begins with proper identification. To accomplish this, our course will help students identify situations and behaviors that could be perceived as employment discrimination. Once this is accomplished, students will learn how to apply specific strategies for eliminating discriminatory employment practices.

We will also examine discrimination against Americans with disabilities. Students will learn what constitutes a disability as outlined by the ADA, and the various types of disabilities. Discrimination laws are enforced by the U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC). The EEOC also provides oversight, guidelines, and coordination of Federal equal employment opportunity regulations, practices, and policies.

AT THE END OF THIS COURSE, THE STUDENT SHOULD BE ABLE TO:
Define discrimination, as outlined by the law.
Identify and eliminate employment decisions based on stereotypes or assumptions about people who are included in any of the protected classifications.
Identify when employment opportunities may be denied to a person based on their relationship to or association with persons included in any of the protected, and then work to eliminate this practice.
Define disability, as outlined by the ADA, as well as list the types of disabilities.
Describe the types of discrimination disabled people are protected against.

WORKPLACE HARASSMENT

COURSE OVERVIEW
The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission defines harassment as unwelcome verbal or physical behavior that is based on race, color, religion, sex (including pregnancy), gender/gender identity, nationality, age (40 or older), physical or mental disability or genetic information. Workplace harassment is a form of discrimination that violates Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 and other federal regulations.

Workplace harassment isn’t limited to sexual harassment. Harassing conduct may include offensive jokes, slurs, name calling, physical assaults or threats, intimidation, ridicule, insults, offensive pictures, and more. The harasser can be a supervisor, co-worker, or non-employee. Interestingly, the victim doesn't have to be the person being harassed—it can be anyone adversely affected by the harassing behavior.

In this course, students will learn how to recognize and address workplace harassment, as well as employ common prevention strategies to keep the workplace free from harassment. They will learn how to analyze their own behavior to ensure it is appropriate at all times, and the importance of promoting a culture of mutual respect in the workplace.

AT THE END OF THIS COURSE, THE STUDENT SHOULD BE ABLE TO:
Define workplace harassment in all its forms.
Understand the devastating effects workplace harassment can have on victims, witnesses, and organizations.
Recognize and address workplace harassment.
Know common prevention strategies against workplace harassment.
Analyze his own behavior to ensure it is appropriate at all times.

WORKPLACE DIVERSITY & INCLUSION

COURSE OVERVIEW
If your assumptions about certain groups of people based on things you have heard from others, in school, the media, or the movies; then it is possible that some of your assumptions are incorrect—at least for some people. Oftentimes, some of the beliefs we hold are the result of our cultural conditioning. They determine if we will seek rapport with individuals who are different from us.

In this course, students are encouraged to accept that although we are human and will probably make some mistakes, we all need to work to become more aware of your inner thoughts and feelings, and how they affect our beliefs, actions, and inactions. Students are taught to separate the facts from their previously held assumptions, opinions, theories, and suppositions so they can make judgments based only on that which is true. We inspire students to engage in honest dialogue with others about race, which may be difficult, risky, or uncomfortable, and to seek out portrayals of different races that are both realistic and positive.

As we gain more awareness and knowledge about groups different than ours, not only will our stereotypes lessen, but we will also become better equipped to educate and challenge others about their stereotypes.

AT THE END OF THIS COURSE, THE STUDENT SHOULD BE ABLE TO:
Define the term "Worplace Diversity".
Understand the devastating effects stereotypes can have on ourselves and others.
Spend time looking, objectively, at their own attitudes and behaviors that may contribute to discrimination within an organization.
Evaluate and work to prevent their use of terms, phrases, or behaviors that may be perceived by others as degrading or hurtful.
